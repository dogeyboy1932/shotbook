PART 1 · INTRO  (to camera)  — [0:00–1:00]
============================================================

Hey — we're [TEAM / NAMES], and we built [PRODUCT NAME].

It's an interactive book-to-video platform: highlight any passage and get a
cinematic clip that's true to the scene.

Why this matters. Two things. One — reading is work; picturing a whole world in
your head is a real barrier for a lot of people. Two — there's a flood of AI video
right now, but almost none of it is grounded in real source material. And studios
are sitting on a century of books they'd love to adapt but can't cheaply test.

Our take is that the edge isn't a fancier video model — it's the data underneath
it. If you actually track everything happening in a story, you can generate video
that's faithful to the book, not just loosely inspired by it. Let me show you.

So this is the product, running right now. Here's a book. I'm going to highlight a

Notice what's in that shot — the right characters, the right setting, the right

PART 3 · PIPELINE  (back to camera)  — [2:30–4:00]
============================================================

So how does that work. The one thing to take away: the model isn't our edge — the
data is. There are two pieces.

(Screen: raw text -> pipeline -> structured "state" panel; 8 H100s lit up.)

First, ingestion — and this runs. We take raw book text and walk the whole story
start to finish, tracking its state at every point — who's alive, who's in the
scene, where they are, what just happened, what each character wants. We run that
across eight H100s for the entire book.

Now, people assume this is just RAG. It's not, and this is the whole difference —
and the new idea here. RAG pulls passages that are similar to your query; it has
no idea where you are in the story, so it'll happily feed the model a character
who died fifty pages ago. We don't do that. For any line you pick, we resolve the
exact state of the world at that point in the timeline — the actual current truth
of the story, not similar text. That's what kills plot holes.

(Screen: highlighted line + state -> prompt -> open-source video model -> clip.)

Second, generation. We bundle the selected text with its resolved state into one
self-contained prompt and hand it to an open-source video model. And here's the
part I want to stress — we didn't touch the model's architecture and we didn't
retrain it. We made it better purely with a smarter harness at inference time:
more compute, better context, stronger prompting. So as the open models improve,
we improve with them, for free.


============================================================
PART 4 · CLOSE  (to camera)  — [4:00–5:00]
============================================================

We think this helps three kinds of people.

The first is just everyday readers. A lot of people want to get into a book but
struggle to picture what's going on, or lose track of who's who. Being able to
highlight a part and actually see it makes reading easier and more fun, and that's
useful to a huge number of people, not a niche.

The second is studios and creators. They can take a book, run it through the same
pipeline, and quickly see what an adaptation could look like before committing real
money to it. A lot of great books never get made into anything just because testing
the idea is expensive. This makes that cheap.

The third is the models themselves. Every book we process turns into clean,
structured data about its world, and that's the kind of grounding the next round of
video models will need to get more accurate.

That last point is really the bigger idea here. Most AI video today just runs free
off a prompt and makes things up. We think the better approach is to give the model
real, structured context about what it's generating, and we'd argue this is a
blueprint for how these models should work going forward, whatever the source
material is.

So to recap: it's grounded in the actual book instead of guessing, it tracks the
whole story so it doesn't contradict itself, and it makes existing video models
better without any retraining.

That's [PRODUCT NAME]. [Closing line — what's next / try it / thanks.]
